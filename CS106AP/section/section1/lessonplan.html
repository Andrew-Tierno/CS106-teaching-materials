Section 1 Lesson Plan

Written by Brahm Capoor with portions shamelessly plagiarized from a handout by Julia Daniel

April 7th, 2019

It's your first CS 106AP section! If you've section led before, sections this quarter are going to look largely like sections in previous versions of CS 106A and CS 106B. We'll be providing section handouts with several problems that you can work through with your section. In keeping with the pedagogy of lecture this quarter, some sections might have more lab-style problems than other sections, but in general, all of your previous training should still hold true.

Each week, you'll get a lesson plan like this one. This is mostly just suggestions as to what I think might make for an effective section. You should feel totally free to disregard this plan, though, if you have another idea. If you're super excited about an alternate lesson plan, feel free to post on the #spr19-ap-section Slack channel in case other people want to try it out!

This week in section, we're hoping you can get to know your section and they get to know each other, and that they understand why section is such a cool and important part of 106AP. In addition, we have some image manipulation problems that you should work through with your section to reinforce their understanding of functions, decomposition and loops.

Getting to know your section & logistics

Feel free to spend a good chunk of time learning names, establishing how section works, and answering logistical questions about the class. Fun icebreakers are a plus. Make it clear that you and the rest of the course staff are here to support them in their learning over the course of 106A. Remind them that you are their primary point-person for questions about the class (although post on Slack or get in touch with Brahm if you ever aren't sure how to answer a particular question from a student!).

It's a good idea to talk about IGs this quarter, explaining that they're a one-on-one time to talk about their assignments and about how the class is going generally. That said, later on this quarter, we might be rethinking the frequency of IGs since the assignments come pretty quickly. Thus, make sure they know to expect IGs for their first assignment, but don't make any particularly strong statements about what to expect after that.

All this being said, if students go down rabbit holes of detailed logistical questions, feel free to have them come talk to you after section so that you leave at least ~20 minutes for Karel.

Lecture Recap

As of your section, there will have been 5 or 6 lectures, and this section covers the first 5. Check out the slides for full details, but here's a quick summary of everything that went down. In addition, make sure to take a look at the exercises students went through in class.

On the digital version of the lesson plan, each of the lecture names is a link to the lecture slides.

  • Lecture #1 - Introduction

    Lecture #1 - Introduction

    We discussed what exactly the relationship between a programmer, code, and a computer is. One of the metaphors we used is that of a musician, the music they're playing, and the instrument they're playing on. In addition, we introduced the idea of a function as an atomic parcel of functionality. We talked about Bit, a Karel-like environment that students were introduced to basic programming principles on. Finally, we spent a little time reviewing syntax errors and bugs, and how to fix them.

    Useful materials: The Bit Reference, linked from the course website or the digital version of this handout. The Bit Reference

  • Lecture #2 - Variables, Loops and Conditions

    Lecture #2 - Variables, Loops and Conditions

    We introduced some of Bit's methods that return values (namely, front_clear() and get_color()) as an initial foray into variables. We used front_clear() as an introduction to boolean values as well as while loops and if statements, and get_color() as an introduction to storing more than just 2 possible values and None as Python's way of indicating the absence of something. Finally, we spent a little time talking about the memory model of variables, although we went into more detail on this in lecture #3.

  • Lecture #3 - Decomposition

    Lecture #3 - Decomposition

    After rounding off our discussion of variables and how they're stored in memory, we moved onto decomposition: specifically, defining our own functions with parameters and calling them. We discussed the two ways of calling functions, either as standalone functions or functions called on objects (although we didn't use the words 'object' or 'method' at any point). We then wrote a more complex program that used all of these ideas.

  • Lectures #4 and #5 - More Decomposition, Range-Based Loops and Images

    Lectures #4 and #5 - More Decomposition, Range-Based Loops and Images

    At the time of printing, we haven't yet had these lectures. That said, the general plan moving forward will be to further discuss decomposition (i.e. return values and so on), introducing range-based for loops and finally introducing the SimpleImage interface, which we'll be using in section this week. Students are not exposed to the idea of images being 2 dimensional arrays, but rather than they simply have coordinates so make sure to use this framing.

    Useful materials: The SimpleImage Reference, linked from the course website or the digital version of this handout. The SimpleImage Reference

Problems

There are 2 problems in this week's section, both of which are intended to stretch student's newfound understanding of images, control flow and functions. My suggestion is to focus on subproblems from the 1st problem, since they're a little heftier and the other problem lends itself better to individual work.

  1. Range-Loop Problems

    These problems are essentially image manipulation problems used to demonstrate basic structures in python programs. If you have time, do the first and last part of this problem. The first allows for students to gain familiarity with the canonical nested for loop used to process images, and the last with parameters and their usage. In addition, it might be worth discussing what students should be looking to understand from the other parts. For example, discussing generally how those solutions would differ from the first part would be an interesting conversation.

  2. Image Puzzles

    In lieu of trace problems on this handout, we have a collection of image puzzles in which students must perform color correction on an exaggerated image by modifying the color components of each of the pixels in the image. In order to do this, they must first look at the images and identify what's wrong with them. Color versions of the images are obviously not available on the black-and-white handout, but are on the online one, alongside a description of the issues for the color-blind and unbothered.

    I don't feel like this is a particularly important one to do in section, especially given that it requires the use of a laptop. That said, you can have an interesting conversation about it. Whereas in the first problem in this section you needed to have a nested for loop to gain access to pixel coordinates, these problems require a single iteration over all the pixels in order to modify their components. Discussing when a nested for loop might be useful as opposed to a linear iteration would be useful for students.
